Tabletop RPGs

I'm a big fan of tabletop RPGs and board games, and I often DM Dungeons and Dragons, although I've also run some other systems and have played a pretty wide range. I've recently started producing content: first writing up adventures that I've run a couple times, and then, in a combination of boredom and inspiration during the quarantine, I've started making my own version of D&D that addresses all my pet peeves. Recognizing that this is a very ambitious project that I'll almost certainly never finish, I'm starting by making it instead a bunch of setting content and general standalone thoughts on D&D.

Why D&D? People often say that in this day of indie RPGs, most of which are more elegant and polished, why would you play something as clunky as D&D? And I still do enjoy non D&D games - I've been playing a variety of PbtA games lately, and DMing a play by post game of Heart. There are a lot of game styles out there, and I reccommend trying a variety. But D&D does one thing very well, and that's being a giant pile of miscellaneous fiddly stuff. A lot of people hate it, for that reason. But I like it.

My grandiose goal is to one day make my own D&D variant, but for now, I'll use D&D 5e for examples of how this content can be applied. However, the ideas aren't edition specific.

How is this new setting different? I've realized that when people say something is "low magic" or "realistic" they often mean something very different than what I mean. I want things to be consistent. I want to deeply explore the impact of magic on the world. I want non-magical characters that are complex. I don't want non-human races that are all culturally identical, or Medieval Europe with magic dropped on top. I want to inject my hobbies about traditional crafts into this setting, so expect some really detailed considerations of fabric production to subtly rear their heads.
